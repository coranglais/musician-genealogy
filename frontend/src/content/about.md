# The Musician Genealogy Project

Musical knowledge travels person to person — teacher to student, generation to generation. The Musician Genealogy Project exists to map that transmission: every instrument, every tradition, every teacher, regardless of fame or prestige.

We believe the studio teacher whose students never grace a concert hall is as much a part of music's living tradition as the celebrated pedagogue. This project is for all of them.

MGP is a community-built resource, free and open. If you studied with someone, you belong here. Add yourself. Add your teacher. Help us trace the roots.

*A note on what this project documents: MGP records pedagogical relationships — who studied with whom, where, and when. Inclusion in the tree documents educational lineage, not endorsement. We recognize that not all teacher-student relationships were positive ones. If you have concerns about your own listing, please [contact us](/contact).*

## How This Project Started

The Musician Genealogy Project grew out of a simple curiosity: who taught my teacher, and who taught theirs? The idea that musical knowledge passes from person to person across generations — and that these lineages can be traced, mapped, and explored — felt like something the music community deserved to have in one place.

Several people deserve special credit. [Irina Morozova](/musician/96), whose playing and teaching are a constant source of musical inspiration. [Robert Craig](http://www.pianist-genealogy.com/), whose pianist-genealogy.com proved that a single dedicated person could build a meaningful pedagogical reference spanning thousands of musicians. [Danny Cruz](https://www.oboefiles.com/) of oboefiles.com, whose research into the American oboe teaching lineage — and his stated wish for an interactive oboe family tree — was the spark that got this project off the ground. And my oboe teacher [Jim Wilson](/musician/80), who passed away in 2025 and would have loved this site. Every teacher matters, and Jim was one of the best.

This site is maintained by David de la Nuez, an amateur oboist, recorder player, pianist, and harpsichordist based in New York City who serves as principal oboe of the Manhattan Wind Ensemble and the Jackson Heights Orchestra.
